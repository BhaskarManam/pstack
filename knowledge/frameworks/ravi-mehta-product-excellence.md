# Ravi Mehta's Product Excellence Framework

## Core idea

Ravi Mehta frames product management excellence as a system of 12 competencies organized into four categories. Most PMs — even experienced ones — are strong in only a handful. The difference between average and peak performance is not more competencies, but depth in the right ones and the ability to build teams that complement your gaps.

> "Most peak product managers I've seen are only really exceptional in one or two of the four categories, not all of them. The goal isn't to become a 10/10 across the board; it's to know your shape and build the team that fills in what you're not."
> — Ravi Mehta, [How To Become a Peak Product Manager](https://www.ravi-mehta.com/product-manager-skills/), ravi-mehta.com

The companion principle: peak PMs hold themselves accountable to **outcomes over output**. Shipping features is not success. A behavior change in users — measurable, attributable — is success.

---

## The 12 product competencies

Four categories, three competencies each:

### 1. Product execution
*The ability to build exceptional products.*

- **Feature specification** — Translating discovery into clear, testable requirements. Not writing requirements for the sake of documentation; writing specs that reduce ambiguity for the product trio.
- **Product delivery** — Shipping reliably: prioritization, sequencing, scope trade-offs. Delivery is a skill, not just project management.
- **Product quality** — Holding the standard. The best PMs feel the gap between "it works" and "it's right" and don't let that gap close by lowering expectations.

### 2. Customer insight
*The ability to understand and deliver on customer needs.*

- **Fluency with data** — Reading product analytics to understand behavior, not just usage. Finding the signal in the metric, not the metric itself.
- **Voice of the customer** — Continuous discovery: regular conversation with users, not quarterly research. Bringing the user's reality into decisions in real time.
- **User experience design** — PM-level design judgment. Not doing the designer's job — but being able to tell the difference between a UX that respects the user and one that extracts from them.

### 3. Product strategy
*The ability to drive business impact via product innovation.*

- **Business outcome ownership** — Knowing the business model deeply enough to connect product decisions to revenue, retention, and unit economics. Not just building what users ask for.
- **Product vision & roadmapping** — Setting direction beyond the next quarter. A roadmap that implements a strategy; not a strategy that is a roadmap.
- **Strategic impact** — Making bets that could change the competitive position, not just improve the current product.

### 4. Influencing people
*The ability to rally people around the team's work.*

- **Stakeholder management** — Getting buy-in without losing conviction. Navigating the organization without becoming the organization.
- **Team leadership** — Helping the team grow, not just the product. Taking outcome accountability seriously means developing the people who deliver the outcomes.
- **Managing up** — Communicating clearly and earning trust from leadership — without becoming a yes-machine.

## The product strategy stack

Ravi's five-layer hierarchy connects company mission to team execution. Most strategy failures happen because teams conflate layers:

```
Company Mission
    ↓
Company Strategy
    ↓
Product Strategy     ← the PM's core responsibility
    ↓
Product Roadmap
    ↓
Product Goals
```

**Key insight:** Goals should flow *from* the roadmap, not precede it. When teams set goals before strategy, they optimize for the wrong things.

> "Most product teams conflate these layers — the strategy and goals become a jumbled mess. Strategy without goals is a wish. Goals without strategy are a shot in the dark."
> — Ravi Mehta, [The Product Strategy Stack](https://www.ravi-mehta.com/product-strategy-stack/), ravi-mehta.com

## NCT: an alternative to OKRs

Ravi designed NCT (Narratives, Commitments, Tasks) to address the gaps in OKRs:

- **Narrative** — 1–3 sentences: what the team wants to achieve this quarter and why it matters to the business. This is the connective tissue between strategy and execution that OKRs skip.
- **Commitments** — 3–5 measurable outcomes the team plans to hit 100% of. Not stretch goals — actual commitments.
- **Tasks** — The concrete work planned to achieve the commitments. OKRs typically omit this; NCT makes the work visible.

The narrative is the distinguishing piece. It keeps teams from optimizing a metric that's disconnected from the actual strategic bet.

## Outcome accountability in practice

Ravi's diagnostic for whether a team is outcome-driven:

1. **Can the team name the specific behavior change they're trying to create in users?** Not "increase engagement" — which behavior, in which users, in what context?
2. **Do the success metrics pre-date the feature scope, or were they backfilled?** Post-hoc metrics are output metrics in disguise.
3. **What would have to be true for this not to work?** If the team can't name the riskiest assumption, they're not outcome-driven yet.
