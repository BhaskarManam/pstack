# Ikigai: Purpose-Led Building

## Core idea

Ikigai (生き甲斐) is a Japanese concept meaning "a reason for being"—the thing that gets you out of bed in the morning. The modern Western interpretation, popularized by Héctor García and Francesc Miralles, maps it as the intersection of four elements:

- **What you love** (passion)
- **What you're good at** (vocation)
- **What the world needs** (mission)
- **What you can be paid for** (profession)

> "Having a strong sense of ikigai—where what you love, what you're good at, what you can get paid for, and what the world needs all overlap—means that each day is infused with meaning."
> — Héctor García & Francesc Miralles, *Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life* (2016)

The concept originates from the Heian period (794–1185 AD). The "paid for" element is a Western addition; the original ikigai is purely about meaning and daily vitality—not monetization. Both dimensions matter for founders.

## The four circles applied to product building

Most founders start in one circle and ignore the others. Ikigai as a product lens asks: does this product sit at the intersection of all four?

| Circle | Product question |
|---|---|
| **Love** | Does the founder genuinely care about this problem? (Passion sustains through hard periods.) |
| **Good at** | Does the founder have relevant skills, context, or unfair advantage? |
| **World needs** | Is there genuine demand—real pain, not imagined pain? |
| **Paid for** | Is there a plausible path to sustainable economics? |

A product in only two or three circles often fails silently:
- **Love + Good at, but no market need:** A passion project with no customers
- **World needs + Paid for, but no love:** A product you'll abandon the moment it gets hard
- **Love + World needs, but no skill:** A vision without executable strategy

## The missing circle diagnostic

Use the four circles as a diagnostic for founder-product fit, not just product-market fit:

| Missing circle | Symptom |
|---|---|
| Missing **love** | Burnout at first obstacle; product feels like a job |
| Missing **skill** | Repeated execution failures in the same area |
| Missing **need** | Building features users don't request; no organic word of mouth |
| Missing **livelihood** | Founder constantly distracted by financial stress; unable to invest in quality |

## Purpose-led building for solo founders

For solo founders and indie builders, ikigai is especially critical. Without a team to carry momentum through hard periods, the founder *is* the product's sustaining force. If the product isn't aligned with the founder's ikigai, it will stall when external motivation (funding, early traction, excitement) fades.

Ikigai-aligned products share these traits:
- The founder talks about the problem even when no one asks
- The founder's background gives them insight customers can't easily replicate
- The customer problem connects to something the founder has lived or deeply understands
- The economics are designed to sustain the founder, not just the investor

## Application in pstack

- **/bhaskar-review:** Applies the four circles as a lens on the vision or PRD. Asks whether the product is ikigai-aligned for the founder—not just commercially plausible.
- **Challenge (reflection lens):** Surfaces the "missing circle" through a provocation. Example: "If revenue disappeared tomorrow, would you keep building this?"
- **/onboard:** The user's ikigai is captured in `user-profile.yaml` and used to personalize all future reviews and challenges.
