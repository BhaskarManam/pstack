# Shreyas Doshi: Product Sense & the LNO Framework

## About Shreyas Doshi

Shreyas Doshi is a product leader and educator who built his career across Yahoo, Google, Twitter, and Stripe—where he was the fourth PM hired and helped grow the PM function from ~5 to 50+. His frameworks on prioritization, high agency, and product sense are widely used across the industry.

## The LNO Effectiveness Framework

The single most impactful prioritization framework Doshi developed, originally shared as a Twitter thread.

> "This framework improved the quality of my life as a PM and my work more than anything else I've encountered."
> — Shreyas Doshi, [@shreyas on X](https://x.com/shreyas/status/1399061782560350208)

Every PM task falls into one of three categories:

| Category | Definition | Effort level |
|---|---|---|
| **L — Leverage** | Tasks where effort has a 10x–100x return. Quality here compounds. | Maximum — perfectionism is warranted |
| **N — Neutral** | Tasks where effort produces roughly 1:1 return. | Standard — do your job well, don't over-invest |
| **O — Overhead** | Necessary tasks with low return. Exist because the system requires them. | Minimal — complete quickly, don't polish |

The core question for every task: *"How impactful will the result of this task be for my business?"*

The failure mode most PMs fall into: applying equal energy to all three categories. They spend Leverage-level effort on Overhead tasks (perfectionism in the wrong places) and under-invest in the tasks that actually compound.

## High Agency

> "High agency is about finding a way to get what you want, without waiting for conditions to be perfect or otherwise blaming the circumstances."
> — Shreyas Doshi

Great PMs are not victims of process, organizational structure, or resource constraints. They identify the unintended flaws in the company's systems and work to fix them. They treat constraints as inputs to navigate, not reasons to stop.

High agency manifests as:
- Shaping the product strategy rather than executing a dictated roadmap
- Surfacing strategic problems before they become execution crises
- Treating advisors (legal, finance, design) as partners, not gatekeepers

## Most execution problems are strategy problems

One of Doshi's central theses: when teams report "we're struggling to execute," the real diagnosis is usually a strategy problem—an unclear objective, a misaligned bet, or a goal that hasn't been pressure-tested.

Signs a reported execution problem is actually a strategy problem:
- Teams disagree on what success looks like
- Priorities shift every sprint without clear rationale
- Progress metrics are being hit but the product isn't growing

## The three levels of product work

Doshi distinguishes work at three levels:
1. **Product decisions** — what to build (should be metrics-informed, not metrics-driven)
2. **Problem shaping** — which problems are worth solving (prevention > solution for high-leverage problems)
3. **Strategy validation** — is the strategy itself optimal, before asking "are we executing well?"

Great PMs operate at all three levels. Good PMs focus on level one.

## Pre-mortem approach

Before a major launch or decision, Doshi advocates running a pre-mortem: *"Assume this failed. What caused it?"* This surfaces risks and assumptions that optimism bias hides during forward-looking planning.

## Application in pstack

- **/shreyas-review:** Applies LNO thinking ("is this roadmap prioritizing Leverage tasks?"), high agency check ("is the PM/founder shaping strategy or executing someone else's?"), and strategy-vs-execution diagnosis.
- **Challenge (priorities lens):** Uses the LNO framework to challenge whether the team is spending effort in the right category.
