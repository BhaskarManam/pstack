# North Star Metric

## Core idea

The North Star Metric (NSM) is the single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. It sits between customer value and business outcomes: if customers are genuinely getting value, the NSM rises; when the NSM rises consistently, revenue follows.

> "If anyone in your organization can't come into work and articulate how the work they're doing is connected to a sustainable growth strategy, then either you don't have a strategy or you have one that isn't understood."
> — John Cutler, Amplitude

The metric is not a revenue metric (that's a lagging indicator of value, not value itself). It is not a vanity metric. It is the clearest possible signal that customers are getting what they came for.

## The full framework: NSM + inputs

The North Star Metric alone is not enough. The framework has two levels:

1. **North Star Metric** — the single value-capturing metric (e.g., Spotify: time spent listening; Airbnb: nights booked; Slack: messages sent within a team)
2. **Input metrics** — 3–5 leading indicators that product teams can directly influence, which in aggregate drive the NSM

Teams work on inputs, not directly on the star. If you're working on the star itself, you're probably gaming it.

## Characteristics of a good North Star Metric

A strong NSM satisfies all six criteria:

| Criterion | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Expresses value** | Captures the moment customers get what they paid for |
| **Represents strategy** | Reflects the product's theory of growth |
| **Leading indicator** | Predicts future business success before it shows in revenue |
| **Actionable** | Teams can influence it with their work |
| **Understandable** | Any employee can explain it in plain language |
| **Not vanity** | Changes in the metric are meaningful, not cosmetic |

## Common failure modes

- **Using revenue as the NSM:** Revenue is a consequence of value, not value itself. It's a lagging indicator that tells you what already happened.
- **Too broad:** "Active users" tells you nothing about whether users got value. Active for how long? Doing what?
- **Too narrow:** A metric for one feature ignores whether the product as a whole is delivering.
- **No input metrics:** Without inputs, teams don't know what to build to move the star.

## Examples

| Product | North Star Metric | Sample inputs |
|---|---|---|
| Spotify | Time spent listening | % users with a playlist, discovery feature engagement |
| Airbnb | Nights booked | Listings quality score, search-to-book conversion |
| Slack | Messages sent per active team | Teams with >10 members, daily senders |
| Duolingo | DAU (daily active users) | Streak retention, lesson completion |

## Application in pstack

- **Vision:** The North Star section should name *one specific metric* and explain why it captures customer value (not just business value). "Revenue" and "user growth" almost never qualify.
- **PRD:** Success metrics should include the relevant input metrics that the feature is expected to move. Identify which input the PRD addresses and by how much.
