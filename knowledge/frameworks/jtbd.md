# Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

## Core idea

A "job" is the progress a person wants to make under specific circumstances. When someone buys a product, they are *hiring* it to get a job done. The product succeeds when it completes the job reliably; it loses when a better hire comes along.

> "People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."
> — Theodore Levitt (cited by Clayton Christensen)

Jobs are **stable**. Products are not. The job a parent has of helping a child fall asleep at night has existed for millennia. The product hired for that job—a lullaby, a storybook, a white-noise machine, an app—changes constantly.

## Three dimensions of every job

Every job has three dimensions. Missing any one of them produces a shallow product brief.

| Dimension | Question | Example (milkshake study) |
|---|---|---|
| **Functional** | What task is being done? | Get me through a boring morning commute |
| **Emotional** | How does it make the person feel? | Feel full, not guilty, in control |
| **Social** | How does it affect their standing? | Look disciplined, not indulgent |

Christensen's famous milkshake study showed that McDonald's should compete with bagels and bananas—not Burger King milkshakes—because the job (filling a commuter's morning) was the unit of competition, not the product category.

## The Four Forces (why people switch)

People only hire a new product when two push forces outweigh two pull forces:

1. **Push:** Frustration with the current solution
2. **Pull:** Attraction to the new alternative
3. **Anxiety:** Fear of learning something new (against switching)
4. **Habit:** Comfort with the existing approach (against switching)

Products fail to gain adoption not because they lack features, but because the pull doesn't outweigh anxiety + habit.

## How to identify a job

- **Switch interviews:** Interview customers at the moment of purchase. Ask: "What were you trying to get done when you decided to switch?" Work backwards from the switch event.
- **Context before features:** Map the *situation* before mapping the solution. A job is always: *When [situation], I want to [progress], so I can [outcome].*
- **Five Whys:** Ask "why" repeatedly until you reach the motivation beneath the action.

## Common failure modes

- Designing for the **persona** ("busy professional") instead of the **job** ("reclaim mornings before the team needs me")
- Listing **features** as the product rather than **progress enabled**
- Ignoring the **emotional and social dimensions** — these often drive switching decisions more than functional gaps

## Application in pstack

- **Vision target user section:** State the job, not just the persona. "Indie founders who need to ship a vision doc before their next investor call" is a job context. "Solo founders" is a persona.
- **Design brief primary flow:** Map the flow to the job steps, not to UI conventions. Each screen should advance the user toward job completion.
